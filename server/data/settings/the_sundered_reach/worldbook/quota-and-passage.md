## The Quota

The human institutions have decided that exactly fifty thousand elves may be alive within the Reach at any one time, and the [[white-capes|White Capes]] enforce that number with bureaucratic precision. It is called, in the dry language of the licensing offices, the Quota. Among elves it has other names.

The arithmetic of the Quota is simple and monstrous. The total is fixed at fifty thousand. When an elf dies, a single birth is permitted somewhere in the registered population — a death creates a licence to live. When an elf is born without a licence — and births do not wait for paperwork — the balance must be restored: that newborn, or another elf chosen to take its place, is killed. The Capes maintain the rolls, issue the birth-licences, and carry out or oversee the corrections. On paper it is presented as the only humane way to manage a magical population that cannot otherwise be contained. In practice it is a slow, administrative culling that has run for three generations.

## Life Under the Quota

The Quota poisons everything inside elven society. A pregnancy is not joyful news but a problem to be solved before the Capes learn of it — and concealing a birth means someone, somewhere, will be marked for correction in its place, possibly a stranger, possibly a neighbour, possibly a member of one's own household. Communities keep their own secret ledgers shadowing the official ones, trying to predict where corrections will fall, and those ledgers are themselves capital offences. An unexpected death can be a grief or an opportunity, because it opens a licence; an unexpected birth can be a miracle or a death sentence for someone else. Elves learn young not to ask who was taken so that they could be born.

The result is a people defined by paranoia and mistrust. Informers are everywhere, because informing on a hidden pregnancy can redirect a correction away from one's own family. Elders who hold birth-licences wield enormous quiet power and are both revered and feared. Whole communities have fractured over a single concealed birth. The Capes did not need to invent most of this cruelty — they built the system and let elven society tear at itself maintaining it.

## Corruption Among the Regulators

A system that trades in licences to live is a system that runs on bribes. White Capes assigned to Quota enforcement occupy one of the most corruptible posts in the Reach: they can be paid to look the other way at a hidden birth, to record a death that did not happen (creating a licence to sell), to delay a correction, to lose paperwork, or to choose which elf is corrected when a balance must be restored. Birth-licences are bought, sold, forged, and stolen on a black market the Capes officially deny exists and unofficially tax. Some Capes are true believers who take no money and enforce the Quota with chilling fairness; some are functionaries who have simply stopped feeling anything; and some have grown rich on the desperation of a people whose right to exist they personally administer. The rot runs in both directions — there are elven brokers who profit from the licence trade as surely as any Cape, and who have no interest in the Quota ever ending.

## The Adjustment Process

When the Quota requires correction — when births have outpaced deaths and the population stands above its fixed ceiling — the mechanism is formal, scheduled, and administered through the [[concordat|Concordat]]. It begins when the [[bureau-of-adjustment|Bureau of Adjustment]] issues an adjustment decree, naming the number of elves who must die to restore the balance. From the moment the decree is issued, a single year is allowed to complete it.

Within the first week, the Concordat leadership must submit a plan: who is to be adjusted, how, and on what schedule. The plan goes to the Bureau for approval. Once approved, the remainder of the year belongs to the Concordat, which carries out the adjustment itself — selecting the elves, managing the process, and ultimately performing the executions with its own members rather than imperial hands. Throughout, Bureau overseers are attached to the Concordat to ensure the adjustment runs smoothly and on time; they do not do the work, but they watch every part of it, and their reports decide whether the Concordat has succeeded.

The penalty for failure is total. If the Concordat leadership fails in any part of the adjustment — misses the count, misses the deadline, lets the plan collapse, allows the process to spark the unrest it exists to prevent — the Bureau executes the entire Concordat leadership and appoints a new one. This is the leash. The selfish, paranoid elf the Empire installs to run the Concordat knows that their own life is the collateral for every adjustment, and so they will drive the cull forward with a ruthlessness no imperial overseer could match, because they are not killing strangers to enforce a policy; they are killing their own people to stay alive. The Empire has arranged matters so that the cruelty is elven, the blame is elven, and the dying is elven, while the decree — the one act that begins it all — is the only part the Empire keeps for itself.

## The Season of Passage

Elven culture is ancient and rich in tradition, and like many peoples under unbearable circumstances, the elves have folded the worst thing that happens to them into ritual. The adjustment year is observed as the Season of Passage, and it is, in the bleak and particular way of the oppressed, a celebration.

At its heart is an act of inheritance. Each elf marked for adjustment spends the season making a necklace of flowers — woven slowly, over the months they have left — and before the end passes it to a younger elf, or to an elf family that intends to use the freed Quota space for a new child. The necklace is the formal passing-on of a life: the place in the world that the dying elf is vacating, given by hand to the one who will fill it. To receive a Passage necklace is to accept both a gift and a debt, and the families who hold them keep them for generations. An elf can often trace their own right to have been born through a chain of these necklaces, each one marking a stranger who died in the year before they were allowed to live.

The Season ends in a single mass execution. All of the adjusted elves are put to death at the same time, by Concordat members — never by human hands — in observances that are part funeral, part festival, and wholly compulsory. There is music, there is feasting in the days before, there are speeches about continuity and sacrifice and the survival of the people; and then there is the morning when it is done, and the necklaces have new owners, and the rolls balance again. Outsiders who witness a Season of Passage rarely understand what they are seeing. Some find it monstrous that a people would dress their own culling in flowers and song. Elves who have lived it tend to answer, if they answer at all, that the flowers and the song are the only parts the Empire could not take from them.

**Hooks (Adjustment & Passage):** an adjustment decree lands during the campaign and a named NPC the party knows is selected; the party is hired (by an elf, by the Concordat, or by a dissident) to alter who appears on the adjustment rolls; a Passage necklace becomes a clue, an heirloom, or a debt the party inherits; a Concordat leadership running out of year and short of its count, willing to do anything — including hiring the party — to avoid its own execution; an elven resistance plotting to disrupt a Season of Passage, knowing that doing so dooms the entire Concordat leadership and forces the Empire to show its hand directly.

**Hooks (Quota in general):** a forged licence the party is asked to carry; a correction the party can prevent, redirect, or be too late for; an honest Cape and a corrupt one assigned to the same case; an elf NPC who exists only because someone the party will later meet was taken in their place.
