title: The Halfling Sixer

The common folk of the Reach understand the three halfling Powers without ever having read a word about them, because they have a proverb for it. To be put through a "halfling sixer" is to be ruined by a friend so charming you thank him for it, and the saying lays out the six steps of the betrayal in order — three of pleasure, three of ruin, each step the work of one of the Powers, alternating Service, Circus, Guards, and then Service, Circus, Guards again. It is told as a grim joke and meant as a real warning:

1. **He feeds you well.** _([[silver-service|Service]])_
2. **He takes you out for a good time.** _([[circus-of-the-moon|Circus]])_
3. **He pays the bill.** _([[fortune-guards|Guards]])_
4. **He entangles you in a web of schemes you cannot escape.** _([[silver-service|Service]])_
5. **He makes it look like it is all your fault.** _([[circus-of-the-moon|Circus]])_
6. **He gets away with it.** _([[fortune-guards|Guards]])_

The proverb's quiet horror is its symmetry. The same three hands that give you the finest night of your life are the three that take everything afterward, and in the same order, and the order is the point — the Service feeds you and then snares you, the Circus delights you and then turns the story against you, the Guards treat you and then walk away clean.

The term has long since escaped the proverb and entered everyday speech, where it bends to whatever the speaker needs. It names the _process_: "I think I'm on step three," a Reach merchant might say of a deal going suspiciously well, and everyone at the table will know exactly what he means and exactly how frightened he should be. It names the _act_: "he pulled a halfling sixer on her" means he charmed someone into ruin and came out clean, whether or not a single halfling was involved. And it names the _person_: to call someone "a sixer" is to call them a charming, generous, patient predator — the kind who buys the drinks and means to collect. To say a man has been "sixed" is to say he is finished and does not know it yet. The wise claim to refuse a halfling's hospitality at step one. Almost no one actually does, because step one is genuinely wonderful, and that has always been the trap.

The sixer is the proverb's gloss on what the [[oath-of-non-competition|Oath of Non-Competition]] looks like from the outside: not three rival cartels but one body with three hands, each playing the part the others cannot, working in alternating sequence on the same mark. The setting's understanding of halfling intrigue is built on this.
