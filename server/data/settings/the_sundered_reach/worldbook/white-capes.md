The White Capes are the magical regulatory arm of the human institutional order — state-sanctioned, state-funded, and in practice answerable to no one. Their formal remit is to enforce the licensing laws around magical practice: hunting unlicensed casters, confiscating unregistered magical items, investigating outbreaks of inherent magic, and policing the registered population of elves and other magical peoples — including administering the Quota, the fixed cap of fifty thousand living elves they maintain through licensed births and enforced corrections (see [[quota-and-passage]]). Their informal remit is broader and considerably uglier.

They are easy to identify on purpose. Every Cape wears a heavy white wool half-cloak, a steel ring at the throat, and gloves of stitched grey leather; the cloak is meant to be visible at a distance, the ring is reputed to be warded against magical detection, and the gloves are for handling things they have decided no one else should touch. They travel in pairs or small companies and are met with the same kind of silence that follows a tax collector or an executioner into a room.

Founded in the chaos of the post-Sundering decades as a reaction to a wave of magical disasters, the Capes were initially a popular institution — they did, demonstrably, stop a number of bad things from happening. Three generations later their power has metastasised. They confiscate without warrant, detain without charge, and execute without trial when they judge the threat severe enough. In the current crisis, with every spell theoretically capable of drawing something through the Sundering, no political faction will publicly oppose them. A handful do so privately. Most do not.

The Capes hold absolute authority over everything magical within the Empire: who may cast, what may be enchanted, which items may exist, and — through the Quota — which magical peoples may live and in what numbers. They are a human-only faction; no dwarf, halfling, or member of any other people is permitted to join, which means the power to regulate magic sits entirely in human hands. Their remit is governed not by the ruling kingdoms jointly but by one human faction that the kingdoms have agreed not to challenge.

Their relationship to the party will be central. Any spellcasting PC is, by default, either licensed (and on a leash), unlicensed (and a target), or an elf or other inherent caster (and a target by existence). A licensed caster who triggers too many [[reckless-casting|Consequence rolls]] in public will attract their attention. An unlicensed caster who triggers any will. The Capes are not stupid, not uniformly cruel, and not without internal dissent — but they are the closest thing the Reach has to a unified power, and they have decided that the door the empire opened will be closed, by whatever means necessary.

## Playstyle — The Cape Campaign

A campaign in which the party _are_ White Capes plays less like a war and more like a series of investigations. The Capes are sent where magic has gone wrong: a haunted manor, an artifact that should not exist, a village where something keeps being seen at the treeline, an unlicensed caster whose spells have started attracting things. Each assignment is a case. The party arrives, gathers evidence, interviews frightened locals who are hiding something, narrows down what they are actually dealing with, and only at the end confronts it — by capture, by confiscation, by ritual binding, or by force.

The tonal touchstone is the paranormal-investigation genre — the rhythm of _ghost-hunter_ and _haunting_ stories rather than the dungeon crawl. The dread builds slowly. Most of a session is investigation, atmosphere, and mounting wrongness; the actual magical entity may not appear in full until the climax, and is more frightening for the wait. This structure is also what lets a low-level Cape party survive hunting magical beings: they are investigators first and combatants last, and a clever party solves most cases without ever rolling initiative.

The work of a Cape falls into three modes, the order's own shorthand for what every case demands.

### The Three R's of the White Cape

- **Reveal.** _(Investigation of magical phenomena.)_ The work of seeing. Hauntings, wards gone wrong, thin places where the Sundering presses through, objects and events behaving as they should not. Before a Cape can act, they must establish what is actually happening — name the thing, trace its source, separate the magical from the merely frightened. Every case begins in the Reveal. A Cape who acts before they see is how new disasters are made.
- **Recover.** _(Artifact recovery and the hunt for magical beings.)_ The work of securing. Whatever is dangerous and is _not_ a person — the relic that should have stayed buried, the creature that slipped through or woke up — is the Cape's to find, identify, and contain. Object or being, the case has the same shape: locate it, learn what it is, and hold it by confiscation, cage, binding, or seal. Force is the last tool, never the first, because the things a Cape recovers are rarely stopped by violence and often made worse by it.
- **Regulate.** _(Capture of rogue spellcasters, and the Quota.)_ The work of accounting. Every practitioner watched, every working weighed, every unlicensed caster found and detained — and, through the Quota, every magical life numbered. This is the duty that turns the Cape from protector to enforcer, because the things being counted and caged are people. It is the work the order is proudest of and the work that damns it, and a Cape learns early that the law leaves them no discretion exactly where their conscience most demands it.

The three modes are also the order a case runs in and a ladder of rising moral weight: a Cape _Reveals_ first (what is this?), then _Recovers_ if the danger is a thing, or _Regulates_ if the danger is a person. Reveal is about truth, Recover is about things, Regulate is about people — and it is no accident that the order's tidy self-image, _we Reveal, we Recover, we Regulate_, hides all of its horror in the third word.

The Cape campaign's engine is the tension between the procedure and the truth. The job has a correct answer — find the anomaly, neutralise it, file the report — and the party will keep discovering that the correct answer and the right thing are not the same. The badge gives them authority over every case; it does not give them a clean way to use it.

### The Tenets of the White Cape

**Reveal.**
See before you act.
Name what hides.
Do not look away.

**Recover.**
Hold what must not be held.
Bind what cannot be killed.
Force is the last tool, never the first.

**Regulate.**
Watch every hand.
Weigh every voice.
Keep the count.

_The door must be closed by any means. We are the means._
