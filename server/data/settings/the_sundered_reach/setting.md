# Setting 1 — The Sundered Reach

This file holds the **core canon** of the setting — the premise, the history, the high-level shape of imperial politics, and the four ruling peoples. Specific factions, named NPCs, named systems (the Quota, Reckless Casting), and named events are documented as individual dossiers in `worldbook/`, fetched on demand via the `lookup_worldbook` tool. The pointers in each section below name the worldbook entries that cover the detail.

## Premise

For most of its long history, the world was quiet. Three peoples lived in it — humans, dwarves, halflings — and none of them knew the word for magic. Then the humans went looking, and found the gnomes, and the gnomes taught them, and the world began to fill with sound.

A century ago that sound became too loud. Something heard it, and answered. The gods left without explanation. The gnomes left without farewell. The empire that had made the noise fell into its own ruins, and the ruins are still there, and so is whatever the noise summoned.

What stands now is the New Empire — Arcania and its junior kingdoms — built on a single promise: that the world will never be that loud again. Magic still works in the Reach. Every spell is a knock on the same door. The new empire hunts anyone who knocks too hard. And the adventurers picking their way through the ruins of the old one are walking, one careful step at a time, the road that ended the world the first time.

## History

### The First Age — The Original Peoples

The three original peoples — humans, dwarves, and halflings — evolved alongside one another across a long quiet age, none aware for most of it that the others existed. Theirs was a sparsely populated world. Dwarves had begun to settle into the bones of the mountains, working metal and stone with patient craft; halflings had taken root in scattered green valleys, building nothing that could not be repaired by hand; humans were spread thinly across the lowlands in small, restless settlements that founded and failed and founded again. None of the three practiced magic, and none of them needed to. The gods walked among each of them openly in this age, and the world, for each people in its own quiet corner, was small and slow and largely understood.

By a point in their development roughly comparable to the eleventh century of our own world — iron tools, written language, walled towns, the first true kingdoms — the three peoples began to find one another. It was humans who did most of the finding. Numerous, ambitious, and restless in a way the others were not, they spread fast and wide across the lowlands and into the foothills, and within a few generations the dwarves of the mountains and the halflings of the green valleys had gone from rumours to neighbours. The contacts that followed were not always peaceful, but they were not catastrophic either: there was a great deal of empty land, and three peoples meeting in it had room enough to learn each other without immediately needing to fight.

### The Coming of the Gnomes

The gnomes were not found until the humans had spread far enough to find them. Where they had been until then, no one is sure — buried in hollow hills, hidden behind veils the gods had drawn across them, simply living in places no one had yet thought to walk. The records of the time are confused. Some say a single gnome stepped out of a hollow hill on a midsummer morning and the rest followed; others that human scouts pushing into unmapped country came over a ridge and saw, for the first time, lit windows in valleys that no map had ever named. What is consistent across every account is this: the gnomes had been there longer than the records of any of the other peoples could reach, they were inherently magical in a way nothing else in the realm was, and they were curious, and they were patient.

For a long generation the contact was cautious on both sides. Then the gnomes began to appear — first as travellers on roads no one remembered building, then as scholars in human courts, then as neighbours in towns that had never had a gnomish quarter before. Whatever caution had held them apart in those first years, it did not last. The gnomes were curious about humans most of all.

### The Teaching

The gnomes taught magic to humans. Whether this was a gift, a bargain, or something less voluntary is one of the central mysteries of the world — the records contradict each other, and the few gnomes who survive will not say. Some human houses tell it as a generous teaching, gnomish tutors patiently lighting candles with a word and showing apprentices the shape of the trick. Others — usually those whose ancestors paid for it — tell it as something more like a contagion, a thing that crawled into ambitious minds and would not crawl back out. Dwarves and halflings were offered the same teaching and, almost universally, refused it. Their reasons differ but their position has never changed across the long ages since: magic is not theirs, and they want no part of it.

### The Spread

Once humans had magic, the world began to fill up. Some of this was deliberate: human mages summoned and bound elementals, called creatures across from neighbouring planes, bred new beasts in their menageries, and woke older things that had been sleeping. Some of it was accidental: every working leaked, every ward weakened the membrane between this world and others, and creatures slipped through into the wild places. Almost every non-original people in the realm today — elves, orcs, goblins, dragons, fiends, fey, the spirits in the deep places — arrived directly or indirectly because of human magic. The original three peoples watched their world become crowded with strangers and, depending on temperament, adapted, retreated, or grew quietly bitter.

### The Imperial Age — The Old Empire

In time, one human dynasty conquered the rest and built the first great empire — the Old Empire, whose ruins now litter the Reach. Its capital was woven through with spellworks; its roads were warded against weather, banditry, and time itself; its archmages had learned to do things the early gnomish tutors had never dreamed of teaching. For three centuries the Old Empire was the most powerful civilisation the world had ever produced. It was also, in retrospect, the loudest — every great working was a hammer-blow on the wall between this world and whatever lay outside it, and the wall had been thinning for a very long time.

### The Sundering

A century ago, the Old Empire performed a working whose purpose is still disputed — some say it was a weapon, others a desperate ward, others an attempt to ascend. Whatever it was, it tore something open. The gods withdrew within a single year, quietly and without ceremony; many of the wards that had held the realm together failed in the decade after; the Old Empire collapsed in the chaos that followed. The gnomes, almost without exception, vanished in the same period — whether they fled, were taken, or were never quite here in the first place is unknown.

For a generation afterward the realm was exactly what the old stories of collapse describe: scattered holdfasts along the imperial roads, ruins full of waking things, and no power large enough to impose order. That interregnum is where the wilderness and the haunted ruins of the present day come from.

### The New Empire

Order returned not through reconquest but through agreement. The three strongest surviving powers — the human kingdom of Arcania, the largest dwarven realm, and the wealthiest halfling factions acting in concert — bound themselves into a single new empire, unipolar and dominant, with every other power in the world reduced to a minor faction at its edges. Its founding principle is reaction against the Sundering: the New Empire exists, above all, to make certain that magic never again grows loud enough to finish what the Old Empire started. Arcania leads; the dwarven realm and the halfling Powers are junior partners, formidable in their spheres but subordinate in the whole. Where the Old Empire was the loudest civilisation the world ever produced, the New Empire has built its entire legitimacy on keeping the world quiet.

## Political Structure

The world is unipolar. The New Empire is the only great power; everything else — independent holdfasts, frontier warlords, free cities, the scattered courts of the non-human peoples, the remnants beyond the old imperial roads — is a minor faction surviving in the Empire's shadow or beyond its reach. There is no rival empire, no balance of powers, no second throne. There is the Empire, and there is everyone the Empire has not yet absorbed.

### The Ruling Body — The Three Kingdoms

The Empire is governed by a compact between the three great powers of the original peoples: the human kingdom of Arcania, the greatest dwarven realm, and the halfling Powers — a bloc of wealthy factions that hold the third seat in concert, since the halflings have no single kingdom or crown. In form it is a partnership. In practice it is human-dominant — Arcania is the largest kingdom, holds the seat of imperial government, and sets the direction of the whole, while the dwarven realm and the halfling Powers are junior partners whose consent is required, whose interests are protected, but whose authority stops well short of Arcania's. The dwarves hold the Empire's infrastructure, fortification, and war-materiel; the halflings hold its trade, credit, and treasury; the humans hold its magic, its laws, and its armies. Each is indispensable. Only one is sovereign.

The factions that operationalise each seat are detailed in the worldbook:

- **Human seat (Arcania).** `arcania` is the kingdom itself. `white-capes` is its magical regulatory arm — human-only, semi-autonomous, the central antagonist for any spellcasting PC, and the playable faction for a Cape-campaign group. `bureau-of-adjustment` is the Empire's legislative + military body, the one great venue where the three ruling peoples sit together.
- **Dwarven seat.** Organised around two great craft-guilds — `lords-of-the-hammer` (static construction: walls, roads, foundations) and `masters-of-the-wrench` (dynamic mechanisms: clockwork, lifts, mills). Allies, both open to all peoples; one of the few genuinely cross-racial institutions in the Empire.
- **Halfling seat.** Three wealthy factions — `silver-service` (restaurants and intrigue), `circus-of-the-moon` (art, assassination, and the propaganda that quietly steers the mood of the entire realm), `fortune-guards` (banking and law) — bound by the `oath-of-non-competition`. The common folk's gloss on how the three Powers work a mark in concert is the `halfling-sixer` proverb.
- **Elven seat (under imperial administration).** The elves are not partners but governed peoples — represented to the Empire by the `concordat`, which functions in practice as their own secret police. The administration of the elven population is documented under `quota-and-passage`.

## Peoples of the Reach

### Dwarves — The Great Engineers

Dwarves are the master builders of the realm. They forge the weapons and armour that fantasy expects of them — pattern-welded blades, scaled mail, helms with the inheritance-marks of a clan stamped along the brow — and the cities of the Reach still buy from dwarven smiths first when they can afford to. But dwarven craft is broader than the forge: they are the realm's civil and mechanical engineers, the people who design the aqueducts, the lift-shafts, the lock systems on the great rivers, the clockwork in the holdfast gates. When a human city wants something built that has to last, they hire dwarves. When they want something built that has to think — a counterweight, a pressure-valve, a mechanism — they hire dwarves and pay extra.

Dwarves do not practice magic and never have. Their position has not changed since the gnomes first offered: they consider magic an unreliable, undisciplined craft, and they take a quiet professional satisfaction in the fact that everything humans build with spells now needs dwarven engineering to hold it together. Most dwarves in the Reach are middle-class to wealthy, often clannish, and largely indifferent to human politics so long as the commissions keep coming. Their craft is organised through two great guilds — see worldbook `lords-of-the-hammer` and `masters-of-the-wrench`.

### Halflings — Masters of Trade

Halflings are the merchants, brokers, and bankers of the Reach. They run the caravan routes between the holdfasts, hold the ledgers of the trade-houses, and quietly own a significant fraction of the realm's productive land through mortgages, leases, and obligations the original owners no longer fully understand. The typical halfling rarely picks up a weapon and almost never holds a title — they don't need to; they lend money to the people who do. (The notable exception lives under the tents of the Circus of the Moon; see worldbook `circus-of-the-moon`.)

Halflings do not practice magic either, and unlike the dwarves they were never very interested. Their position has always been pragmatic: magic creates as much instability in markets as it removes, and a halfling counting-house would rather lend to a wizard than employ one. In the present crisis their leverage has only grown — the holdfasts run on credit, and the credit runs through halfling hands. Their power at the imperial level is held not by a kingdom but by three factions, the Halfling Powers — see worldbook `silver-service`, `circus-of-the-moon`, `fortune-guards`, the `oath-of-non-competition` that binds them, and the `halfling-sixer` proverb that names how they work a mark in alternating sequence.

### Humans — Generalists and Monopolists

Humans are everywhere and do everything. Most live lives that would be recognisable to any medieval European peasant or classic-fantasy townsfolk — farming, smithing, soldiering, scribing, dying young. The realm's politics are mostly human politics; the holdfast lords are mostly human lords; the bandits, the priests, the guildsmen, the city-watch are mostly human. Humans are the generalists — never the best at any single thing, but present in numbers nothing else can match.

What humans do monopolise is institutional magic. Every legitimate magical institution in the Reach — every academy, every chartered conclave, every licensed practitioner — is human-run and human-staffed. Only a small minority of humans actually cast (the temperament for it is rare and the training is brutal), and access to that training is controlled by a handful of factions. Independent practice is illegal almost everywhere. In the century since the Sundering, magic has gone from imperial monopoly to tightly regulated guild craft, and the people who hold the licences hold a great deal of power.

### The Inherently Magical Peoples

Elves, and other peoples whose magic is inherent rather than learned, live as second-class citizens across most of the Reach. Their magic cannot be regulated the way a human caster's can — they do not choose to cast in the same sense — and the dominant human institutions treat that as a permanent threat rather than a fact about them. They are subject to registration, restricted from most professions, often confined to specific quarters, and frequently the first targets of the White Capes when something goes wrong.

Of all the inherently magical peoples, elves are the most numerous, the most visible, and the most tightly controlled. The Empire administers them through a fifty-thousand-life population cap called the **Quota**, an annual cull called the **Adjustment**, and the elven body that operationalises both — the **Concordat**. The machinery is documented in worldbook `quota-and-passage`; the Concordat itself in worldbook `concordat`.

## Key Mechanics

Two mechanical systems shape play in the Reach. Both are documented in the worldbook, not here:

- **Reckless Casting & Consequences** — every spell is a knock on the same door that ended the world the first time. Each cast triggers a Consequence Check; failed checks roll on Minor / Moderate / Severe tables, severity rising with spell level and recent casting. The system is the in-game reason the White Capes exist, the in-game reason elves whose magic is inherent live in quiet existential horror, and the mechanical floor under every spell in play. See worldbook `reckless-casting`.
- **The Quota and the Season of Passage** — the fifty-thousand-life cap on elves, the annual decree that adjusts the rolls when births outpace deaths, the year-long Concordat-administered cull, and the ritual the elves wrap around it. See worldbook `quota-and-passage`.

## Minor Factions

Minor factions are not single, unified powers like the White Capes or the Halfling Powers. They are _types_ of faction — recurring categories that appear across the Empire in many separate, small-scale incarnations. Each individual minor faction has its own leadership, motives, and territory, but none of them is detailed in this document. The setting describes only the type; the specific crews, packs, lairs, leaders, and grievances are for the GM to invent as the campaign needs them.

Documented types so far in the worldbook: `bandits`, `monsters`.

**Other minor faction types to develop:** cults, smuggler rings, refugee bands, holdfast militias, scavenger / ruin-delver crews, fanatical preachers' movements, free cities and frontier councils, surviving fragments of the Old Empire. TBD.
